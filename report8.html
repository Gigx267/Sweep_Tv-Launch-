NIGERIA IN CRISIS: HUNGER, FEAR, AND SOARING COST OF LIVING PUSH MILLIONS TO THE BRINK

Published April 5, 2026 · Special Investigative Report | By Sweep TV Crisis Desk — Abuja, Kaduna, Maiduguri
RED ALERT: Nigeria is currently facing one of its most difficult moments in recent history, as a combination of rising insecurity, economic hardship, and increasing fuel prices continues to put unbearable pressure on citizens across the country. From Kaduna State to Plateau State, and stretching to Borno State, communities are under siege. This is the untold story of a nation stretched to its breaking point.

In the sprawling markets of Kano, exhausted mothers pick through leftover vegetables. In the internally displaced camps of Maiduguri, children search for scraps. In the urban centres of Lagos and Abuja, the middle class is vanishing overnight. Nigeria, Africa's largest economy, is witnessing a perfect storm of insecurity, hyperinflation, and policy failure — pushing over 100 million people into extreme hardship. This special report examines the human cost, the systemic failures, and the urgent path forward.

Insecurity: Communities Under Siege

Reports of bandit attacks, kidnappings, and violent killings have become a near-daily occurrence, forcing families to abandon their homes and live in fear. In many rural areas, residents no longer sleep peacefully, uncertain of what the night may bring. From Kaduna State to Plateau State, and stretching to Borno State, entire communities have been hollowed out by terror.

“We fled with nothing” — Aisha’s story, Zamfara State

“Bandits came at midnight. We ran into the bush with only our children. Now we live in a makeshift camp. My husband was killed last month. I have six children and no food. The government never came.” — Aisha, 34, displaced from Zurmi LGA.

In the North-East, Boko Haram and ISWAP remnants continue to ambush farmers and travellers. In the North-West, armed banditry has made farming nearly impossible — food production has collapsed across several local government areas. Meanwhile, in the South-East and South-South, unknown gunmen and cult violence have disrupted trade and commercial activities. The insecurity is not just a humanitarian tragedy; it is a direct cause of rising food prices as farmers abandon their lands.

Economic Implosion: Fuel, Transport & Starvation Wages

At the same time, the economic situation continues to deteriorate. Fuel prices have surged past ₦1,200 per litre in many states — and with it, the cost of nearly everything else. Transportation fares have doubled in some areas, food prices have skyrocketed, and basic necessities are now out of reach for millions of Nigerians.

Fuel subsidy removal's unintended toll

While the removal of fuel subsidies was touted as a necessary economic reform, the absence of adequate palliative measures has crushed low-income families. A trip that cost ₦500 a year ago now costs ₦2,000. The price of a bag of rice has tripled. Bread — once a staple — has become a luxury for millions.

In markets and homes across Nigeria, families are making painful sacrifices. Parents are skipping meals so their children can eat, while others are forced to survive on the barest minimum. What was once considered a normal standard of living is now a luxury many cannot afford. In Lagos, queues for discounted "Sunday rice" have turned deadly. In Kano, malnourished children fill hospital beds.

“My shop is gone. I sold fabrics for fifteen years. Now I sell firewood just to buy garri for my grandchildren. The government says things will improve — but we are dying now.”
— Madam Grace, market trader, Jos (Plateau State)

Small Businesses Crushed: The Unemployment Spiral

Small businesses are also feeling the impact. With high fuel costs and unstable power supply, many entrepreneurs are struggling to keep their businesses afloat. Some have shut down completely, leading to job losses and increased unemployment, further deepening the crisis. The manufacturing sector has shed thousands of jobs, while transportation unions report that 40% of commercial drivers have parked their vehicles due to unaffordable fuel and spare parts.

The ripple effects are catastrophic. Young Nigerians who once relied on informal work — from okada riding to phone repairs — are now idle. Crime rates in urban centres have spiked as desperation mounts.

Frustration boiling over online and on the streets

Social media hashtags like #HungerInTheLand and #NigeriaIsBurning have trended for weeks. Protests have erupted in Ibadan, Minna, and Kano — met with heavy-handed police responses. The feeling of helplessness is palpable. “We voted for change, but change brought hunger,” said one protester in Abeokuta.

Government response: Promises vs. Reality

Despite repeated assurances from the government, many Nigerians are calling for urgent and practical solutions—not just promises. They want improved security, stable fuel prices, affordable food, and policies that will directly impact their daily lives. The much-touted student loan scheme, consumer credit, and CNG conversion program have yet to reach the millions who need immediate relief. The Naira's free fall has not been arrested, and the Central Bank's monetary tightening has done little to tame inflation.

Opposition lawmakers have called for a state of emergency on food security. Civil society organizations are demanding a comprehensive social safety net. But time is running out.

The Human Toll: Hunger, Fear & Lost Hope

Malnutrition ward, Maiduguri — Children who have forgotten how to smile

At the state specialist hospital in Maiduguri, Dr. Fatima Bintu says she has never seen such severe wasting. “We are admitting twice as many children as last year. Mothers bring them too late — some die on the way. The cost of therapeutic milk has tripled. We are working with almost nothing.”

The reality on the ground is clear: People are hungry. People are afraid. People are struggling. Nigeria, a country rich in natural and human resources, is now witnessing a period where its greatest asset—its people—are under severe strain. The psychological toll is immeasurable: suicide rates are climbing, domestic violence reports have surged, and hope is becoming a scarce commodity.

What must change — urgent demands from civil society
  • Immediate release of grain reserves & price controls
  • Subsidized public transport & fuel price regulation
  • Massive investment in security to reopen farming corridors
  • Direct cash transfers to vulnerable households (not conditional loans)
  • Emergency food distribution via local government networks
  • Accountability for billions spent on palliatives with no impact
#RenewedHopeOrRenewedHunger #NigeriaCannotWait
In global context: Nigeria now has one of the highest multidimensional poverty rates in the world — surpassing even conflict-ridden nations. According to the World Bank, over 40% of Nigerians live below the national poverty line. Yet the country remains a top oil producer. The paradox of poverty in the midst of plenty has never been more glaring.
Editorial: A nation at a crossroads

This is not just a political or economic crisis — it is a moral one. The suffering unfolding across Nigeria is not inevitable; it is the result of policy failures, corruption, and a disconnect between the ruling class and the governed. As the 2027 elections draw closer, the question for every Nigerian is no longer about party affiliation, but about survival. Sweep TV will continue to hold power accountable and amplify the voices of the voiceless. The time for action is now.
